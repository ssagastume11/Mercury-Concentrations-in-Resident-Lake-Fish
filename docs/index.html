Introduction

Mercury contamination in aquatic ecosystems poses significant risks to wildlife and human health. This analysis investigates mercury concentrations in fish from Lake Clark National Park and Preserve (LACL) using data collected in 2019 and 2020. The project follows the Google Data Analytics framework to assess spatial differences, temporal trends, and species-specific health impacts of mercury contamination in resident fish.

Scenario

These data were collected as part of the Southwest Alaska Inventory and Monitoring Network (SWAN) freshwater contaminants protocol. The dataset includes biological and lab data for 49 fish sampled from Lake Clark and Kijik Lake. Four species are represented: lake trout, burbot, Arctic grayling, and Arctic char.

Ask

Business Task:
Understand spatial differences, temporal trends, and health implications of mercury (Hg) contamination in fish species across Lake Clark and Kijik Lake in LACL. Use this analysis to support ecological monitoring and inform potential public health decisions.

Key Questions:
- Are there significant differences in total mercury concentrations between the two lakes (Lake Clark and Kijik Lake)? - How do mercury levels vary among fish species, particularly lake trout and burbot? - Are there observable trends between 2019 and 2020 in mercury concentration?

Process

Data Cleaning Steps:

Act

Summary of Key Findings

  • Mercury concentrations vary by lake, with Lake Clark showing higher average levels than Kijik Lake.
  • Among species, Lake trout exhibited the highest mercury concentration on average.
  • There may be slight temporal variation between 2019 and 2020, but more data is needed to confirm trends.
  • A positive relationship appears between fish weight and mercury concentration, particularly in lake trout.

Recommendations

  1. Continue targeted mercury monitoring in Lake Clark and Kijik Lake, with an emphasis on long-term trends and year-to-year variability.
  2. Prioritize species for further study and potential public health advisories due to elevated mercury concentrations.
  3. Share findings to National Park Service ecologists, fisheries biologists, and public health officials to inform conservation and safety strategies.
  4. Collaborate with environmental researchers contributing to mercury levels, such as glacial melt, sediment runoff, or wetland influence.
  5. Expand future monitoring to additional years and nearby lakes to strengthen ecological understanding and assess regional patterns.
  6. Educate local communities about mercury risks in fish consumption and promote responsible fishing and dietary choices.
